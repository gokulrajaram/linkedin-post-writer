# Voice Samples

Four prior LinkedIn posts that represent the target voice. Use these to calibrate tone, rhythm, argument pattern, and vocabulary. Do not copy them: absorb the style.

**Important caveat on punctuation:** These historical samples use em-dashes (—) for asides. The current style has moved away from em-dashes. When drafting new posts, use colons (:) where these samples use em-dashes. Everything else about the voice still applies.

---

## Sample 1 — Contrarian-claim hook, famous anchor example (Moritz/Instacart)

The hardest thing in venture is falling into the pattern matching trap based on prior experience. It's super challenging to keep an open mind and to preserve first principles thinking while evaluating a new opportunity.

This is why I maintain that the best venture deal ever is Mike Moritz's investment into Instacart. Not just because the exit was amazing ($10b public company!) but because Moritz had invested $50m+ into Webvan, the online grocery delivery service that was one of the biggest casualties of the dot-com boom when it filed for bankruptcy in 2001. Twelve years later, Moritz led the series A of Instacart.

To me, the Instacart investment shows the first principles thinking, the intellectual humility, and the emotional resilience that separates great investors.

Most people who lost $50 million watching a grocery delivery company implode would have built a mental scar tissue around the category. "We tried this. It doesn't work. Consumers don't want it. The unit economics are broken." Moritz could easily have justified the pass.

But first principles thinking requires you to decompose the question "did online grocery delivery fail?" and ask why it failed — and whether anything has changed since.

By 2012, the answer was obviously yes. Smartphones meant consumers always had the app in their pocket. GPS made real-time logistics tractable. The gig economy created a flexible labor pool that made asset-light delivery possible. Webvan failed because it tried to own the warehouses, the trucks, and the last mile in an era when the infrastructure didn't exist to support any of it cheaply. Instacart was a fundamentally different business, though it might have looked superficially similar.

In venture, pattern matching is seductive precisely because it's usually right. The trick is knowing when your pattern is a useful heuristic and when it's a cognitive trap. The best investors hold their theses loosely enough that new evidence can update them. Moritz's Instacart investment is the clearest proof of this.

## Sample 2 — Topic+colon hook, behavioral observation, sharp CTA ("If this is you, stop.")

Career advice: Stay long enough to have an impact

I'm seeing many folks who exhibit the following pattern:

- Do a role for 12-18 months
- Change roles
- Repeat

They are "job optimizers", constantly on the lookout for something better, almost from the moment they land in a new role. The purpose of their current role is to help them find their next role.

If this is you, stop. Take a breath. Embrace your current role. In fact, fall in love with it. Throw yourself into learning, building and having impact. You need at least 3-4 years at a company to have real impact. Have impact with measurable outcomes, and the next role will take care of itself. If you do great work at a good company, word will get out and you will never need to look for a job again. Plus, the joy and satisfaction of having meaningful impact is reward in and of itself.

Frank Slootman offers a few other reasons why employers see too many short-tenured jobs as a red flag.

## Sample 3 — All-caps header hook, contrarian take on accepted default, direct founder CTA

EQUITY VESTING

I'm starting to see more founders put into place 6 year vests for founding / early employees, and I think it really aligns incentives around thinking long term and sticking around to build an enduring company.

Founders: don't take 4 year vests as written in stone. Rethink it from first principles, considering what you're trying to build.

4 years made sense in an era when the average time to IPO was 4-5 years. That era is gone. The best companies today take 10-15 years to reach their full potential. If your vest schedule is shorter than your ambition, you've created a misalignment.

A few things worth rethinking:
• Cliff length (discussed above)
• Back-weighted schedules (more equity in years 4-6, not front-loaded; Amazon has done this for many years?)
• Refresh cadence (how do you keep people incentivized post-vest without diluting everyone?)

The vest schedule is a cultural document. It signals how long you think this will take, and how serious you are about people staying for the whole ride.

## Sample 4 — Question hook, framework-naming post (the "dashboard or pipes" framework)

Dashboard or Pipes?

Every startup needs to make a choice: is their product a dashboard product or a pipes product?

Dashboard products are used directly and regularly by end users as their primary interface for accomplishing tasks. The goal for these products is to get customers to live in the product. The primary North Star metric for these companies is active users (daily / weekly / monthly, depending on the natural frequency of customer usage for the category). Facebook's first product (aka Facebook :)) was a dashboard product.

Pipes products are used in the background to process transactions, data, payments, etc, and customers rarely interact with them directly after initial setup. The goal for these products is to for their customers to send as much of their data / payments / etc through them. Their North Star metrics is a volume metric (eg GPV). Databricks' core product is a pipes product.

Companies can have both types of products in their portfolio. For example, ChatGPT is a dashboard product while OpenAI's APIs are a pipes product. However, a given product has to determine which camp it's primarily in.

This choice dictates product development, growth strategy, and org structure. For example, dashboard products require heavy investment in UI/UX polish, engagement features, and retention loops, while pipes products prioritize reliability, throughput, integration breadth, and seamless embedding into customer workflows. Dashboard products have consumer-style growth teams focused on activation and habit formation to grow [DWM]AUs, while pipes products focus on making their product invisible infrastructure that "just works" and on capturing more and more of their end customers' volume.

Startup CEOs: do you know what kind of product your company is building? If so, have you set up your North Star metric, your product development processes and your org structure accordingly?

## Patterns to notice across all four

1. **Every post has ONE anchor example.** Not three. Not a greatest-hits list. One concrete case that the whole argument rests on.
2. **Every post names a framework or cognitive concept.** "Mental scar tissue", "job optimizers", "cultural document", "dashboard vs pipes". The name is sticky.
3. **Every post ends with direct address.** "Founders:", "Startup CEOs:", or a conditional ("If this is you, stop.").
4. **The hook earns the click in one line.** No windup. No "I've been thinking about…".
5. **Specifics everywhere.** $50M. 2001. 12 years. 4 years. 10-15 years. Moritz. Slootman. Amazon. Databricks. Never abstract when a specific would do.
6. **Short paragraphs. Whitespace.** Often one sentence per paragraph. This is the LinkedIn-native rhythm.
7. **Confident but not preachy.** Uses "I maintain", "To me", "I'm seeing" — owns the take as personal without dressing it up as universal truth.
